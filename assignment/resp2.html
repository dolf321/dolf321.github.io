Our Little Cousin Book Series

Hello! Today we will be analyzing three books from the Our Little Cousin series. The series contains a set of 19th-century fictional allegories that portray racist cultural stereotypes of certain ethnicities or nationalities of people around the world to "educate" younger audiences on what the author believes to be these people's culture and traditions. In specific, the 3 books we'll be looking at today are "Our Little Jewish Cousin," "Our Little Arabian Cousin," and "Nabul, Our Little Egyptian Cousin," all downloaded from Project Glutenburg. Let's get to it...

Word occurences

After creating our corpus containing these three books, we ran a view of the most frequently occurring words in these texts with a stopword list to remove any commonly used words. To analyze word occurrences, we create a wordcloud in Rstudio and also display their frequencies to get a better understanding.

We see that George is one of the most frequently used. This is because George is one of the main characters in the Egyptian Cousin book, where he is depicted as an American visiting Egypt. We verify this by running a collocation on the word "George." We also see children are frequently mentioned, which makes sense since the book discusses children as that is their target audience. Another interesting word is "donkey." This is mentioned in every book, primarily in the Egyptian Cousin one. What might seem like an inaccurate depiction of Egypt is the fact that donkeys are mentioned as a more frequent form of transport than other Fauna such as camels (mentioned 50 times) or horses (mentioned 27 times), which were also quite common in Egypt at the time.

Lets compare

From this visualization, we notice a couple distinctive word comparisons. We see that tents, camels, and horses are more frequently mentioned in the Arab book in comparison to the Egyptian book. The text tries to inaccurately connote that Arabs live mostly in the desert and in tents while Egypt has more infrastructure because the words 'built' or 'street' used more frequently in Egypt. An interesting note is the word "Al" which is used in the Arabic language appears more frequently in the Arab book when comparing to the Egyptian one. "Al" means "the" in Arabic and is commonly used to address objects or people in formal Arabic. It is usually used less frequently in Arabic dialects such as Egyptian Arabic, so that might explain the comparison. In comparison between Arabian Cousin and Jewish Cousin, we see Boston is more frequently mentioned in the Jewish Cousin book. This is possibly related to the fact that Boston is home to the fourth largest Jewish community in the United States. We also see God is mentioned much more frequently in the Jewish book. This result is interesting considering religion also plays a big part in Arab culture and language, but God is mentioned only around 0.01% of the time in the Arab books. We immediately see a significant amount of Zionist literature in the Jewish text claiming that it is the word of God to liberate the Jewish community from its cruel Egyptian Arab rulers. This allegory, although fictional, connotates that Jewish communities are oppressed by Arab rulers. Furthermore, the lack of religious representation of Islam in the Arab books, while a significant representation—although one would argue an innacurate one—of Jewish religion in the Jewish book could be used to reason that a bias exists in the texts through potentially favoring Judaism over Islam.

Concluding remarks

In conclusion, through the analysis of the Our Little Cousin series, we identify a political and stereotypical agenda to be read to children in the 1900s. We identified strange portrayals—mostly inaccurate—of Jewish and Arab communities through the common use of stereotypical names and their cultures and traditions.
